It's been almost a year since the release of PCSX2 2.0. Since then, we've released PCSX2 2.2.0, and today, we're excited to release 2.4.0! We're even more excited to show how we've refined what 2.0 brought and then ramped up again – back to bringing you bold new features. We present this chronologically to show you the trajectory we're on and therefore what to expect for the release of 2.6.0 – tentatively scheduled for late 2025.

## PCSX2 2.2.0

PCSX2 2.0 may well have been <a href="https://pcsx2.net/blog/2024/pcsx2-2-release">our biggest single release to date</a>. With several major contributors stepping back, including longtime PCSX2 lead developer refraction, the remaining team – still energized to contribute – confronted reality: for the first time in a long time, we'd delivered what we had to offer in the way of big, bombastic new features. What we resolved for PCSX2 2.2 was to take it out of the furnace, let it cool, and hammer it into something more refined before getting up to speed again in 2.4. For this period of a few months, the word on everyone's mind was "polish".

<PCSX2PRLink authors="TellowKrinkle,F0bes" prNums="11372,11593">
  ### Video Capture improvements (4)
</PCSX2PRLink>

For a realistic window into the early days of 2.2, we're intentionally starting small. PCSX2 has a convenient, built-in video capture tool for gameplay recording. Initially broken when we switched to Qt, what was a barebones, unmaintained "take what you get"-style button quickly became a feature-rich tool that could – for casual users – rival external programs. Early into 2.2 and with polish in mind, remaining video capture bugs became one of our first targets. In two consecutive PRs, PCSX2 team member and macOS expert TellowKrinkle fixed <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_Acceleration_API">VAAPI</a> support on Linux, and PCSX2 lead developer fobes fixed recording with AMD GPUs on Windows by making it so users can now choose a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixel_format">pixel format</a> on a per-codec basis.

<PCSX2PRLink authors="chaoticgd" prNums="10224,11901">
  ### Symbol Parsing Overhaul (4)
</PCSX2PRLink>

Courtesy of contributor Chaoticgd is a massively improved debugger. This overhaul, in the works for almost a year, allows the debugger to read <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIPS_architecture">MIPS</a> debug (`.mdebug`), SNDLL (`.sndata`), and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executable_and_Linkable_Format">ELF</a> (`.symtab`) <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbol_table">symbol tables</a> from a game's executable binary if it has them included. If the `.mdebug` section is available, the debugger can now display information about global variables, local variables, and parameter variables, including data types. This means you can explore data structures in memory as a structured tree. He subsequently added settings that give you fine-grained control over how and when PCSX2 performs symbol analysis passes. A few games that shipped with their debug symbols include <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Pac-Man_World_2">Pac-Man World 2</a></i>, <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Sega_Soccer_Slam">Sega Soccer Slam</a></i>, and <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Fatal_Frame">Fatal Frame</a></i>. ## PCSX2 2.4.0

After 2.0, the 2.2 development cycle let us catch our breath and – for the most part – focus on finer details. Brewing under the surface, though, were fresh ideas and the zeal to bring them to life. We all wanted to get back to innovating, so that's what we did.

<PCSX2PRLink authors="TheTechnician27" prNums="12208">
  ### Custom Real-Time Clock (v2.3.130) (5)
</PCSX2PRLink>

The PlayStation 2 uses a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-time_clock">real-time clock</a> (RTC) to keep track of the date and time in the real world. Games often use the RTC in order to seed pseudorandom number generation (RNG) because of its high variability. Additionally, save files show a timestamp in the BIOS, and these timestamps are sometimes shown in-game too. However, some games use the RTC in a more interactive way. <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Ratchet_%26_Clank:_Going_Commando">Ratchet & Clank 2</a></i> and <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Ratchet_%26_Clank:_Up_Your_Arsenal">Ratchet & Clank 3</a></i> let you teleport to the post-game Insomniac Museum area from 03:00–04:00, bypassing completion checks. <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/The_Simpsons:_Hit_%26_Run">The Simpsons: Hit & Run</a></i>, <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/The_Simpsons:_Road_Rage">The Simpsons: Road Rage</a></i>, <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/SpongeBob_SquarePants:_Battle_for_Bikini_Bottom">SpongeBob SquarePants: Battle for Bikini Bottom</a></i>, and <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Scooby-Doo!_Night_of_100_Frights">Scooby-Doo! Night of 100 Frights</a></i> have holiday-exclusive Easter eggs. <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Need_for_Speed:_Hot_Pursuit_2">Need for Speed: Hot Pursuit 2</a></i> displays the date and time on a camera recording when you get busted. And <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Metal_Gear_Solid_3:_Snake_Eater">Metal Gear Solid 3</a></i>... if you know, you know.

By default, when a user starts a game, PCSX2 sets the RTC to the date and time from the user's desktop. For input recordings which require perfect determinacy to function correctly, we instead used a fixed, arbitrary date and time after the release of <i>Metal Gear Solid 3</i> (which crashes if the date is too old). With the introduction of RTC settings by TheTechnician27, you can now optionally select a custom RTC starting point in the per-game settings (`Game Properties` > `Emulation` > `Real-Time Clock`). This provides niche benefits for regular users (especially <i>Ratchet</i> players and enjoyers of Hideo Kojima's antics), and it brings PCSX2 closer to having a mature suite of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tool-assisted_speedrun">tool-assisted speedrun</a> (TAS) capabilities.

This currently requires you to have your BIOS set to GMT+0 and daylight savings time (DST) set to Summer Time (this is the default PCSX2 creates if no `.nvm` file exists). Additionally, it will only work within the bounds of 2000-01-01 at 00:00 and 2099-12-31 at 23:59, which is the range of an actual PlayStation 2. <PCSX2PRLink authors="refractionpcsx2" prNums="12254">
  ### HDR Optimization (v2.3.135) (2)
</PCSX2PRLink>

Former lead PCSX2 developer refraction (codename: "<a href="https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/OneLastJob">One Last Job</a>") came back from retirement swinging with a major optimization to how PCSX2 handles blending. The PS2 uses <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_dynamic_range">high dynamic range</a> (HDR) different from the kind you're probably used to: its blending unit has 9 bits of accuracy compared to a standard GPU's 8 bits, meaning PCSX2 needs to perform frequent format conversions to handle this inherent hardware discrepancy. This optimization attempts to minimize the number of conversions, considerably improving performance on a handful of games – especially on the Direct3D11 and 12 renderers. For more details, refraction did <a href="https://www.readonlymemo.com/pcsx2-render-target-hdr-performance-graphical-fixes/"> an interview</a> with emulation news site Read Only Memo. Below is a chart showing the performance uplift in <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Sly_2:_Band_of_Thieves">Sly 2: Band of Thieves</a></i> and <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Big_Mutha_Truckers">Big Mutha Truckers</a></i>.

<PCSX2PRLink authors="F0bes" prNums="12255">
  ### Signed macOS Binaries (v2.3.160) (3)
</PCSX2PRLink>

macOS requires all code binaries to be signed. If not, macOS' <a href="https://support.apple.com/guide/security/gatekeeper-and-runtime-protection-sec5599b66df/web">Gatekeeper</a> will report that the program cannot be opened because it is from an unidentified developer, thus it "cannot verify that this app is free from malware". This was a headache for users, who needed to explicitly enable a setting to use unsigned programs. As such, PCSX2's lead developer, fobes, worked to get PCSX2 formally signed. This means that when you download and run PCSX2 on macOS, it will now proceed normally.

<PCSX2PRLink authors="GovanifY" prNums="12333">
  ### Wayland by Default (v2.3.168) (5)
</PCSX2PRLink>

In the Linux world, the last remaining hurdles to PCSX2 running on <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wayland_(protocol)">Wayland</a> by default are finally gone! Thanks to upstream fixes in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qt_(software)">Qt</a> 6.9 and some clever work by team member GovanifY, the `I_WANT_A_BROKEN_WAYLAND_UI` flag is history! This does not mean that no Wayland-specific bugs exist, but it means we've failed to find any we consider showstoppers. If your desktop environment uses <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X_Window_System">X11</a>, nothing will change, and we'll continue accepting reports for X11-specific bugs for the foreseeable future. If you find a Wayland-specific bug, the `QT_QPA_PLATFORM=xcb` environment variable can take you back to XWayland while we work to fix it.

  ### New Upscaling Fix (4)
</PCSX2PRLink>

Thanks again to former PCSX2 lead developer refraction, PCSX2 now has a brand-new tool in its arsenal to fix misalignment issues caused by upscaling. Based on the existing Align to Native (AtN) half-pixel offset (HPO) option, Align to Native with Texture Offset (AtNTO) often combines the best of AtN and another HPO option called Special (Texture) to make a lot of games much sharper when upscaled. Thanks to a massive effort by GameDB specialist JordanTheToast, PCSX2 now applies this new setting automatically to over 100 games. These include <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Ace_Combat_04:_Shattered_Skies">Ace Combat 04: Shattered Skies</a></i>, the <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Sly_Cooper">Sly Cooper</a></i> trilogy, <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/God_of_War">God of War</a></i> and <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/God_of_War_II">God of War II</a></i>, and <i><a href="Shadow_of_the_Colossus">Shadow of the Colossus.</a></i> Below is a before and after of ''Ace Combat 04''.

<PCSX2PRLink authors="TheLastRar" prNums="12311">
  ### SDL3 Upgrade (v2.3.198) (4)
</PCSX2PRLink>

The right answer to "What does <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_DirectMedia_Layer">SDL</a> do?" is "What doesn't it?", but PCSX2 uses it to handle gamepads and audio output. In January 2025, SDL's release of its newest major version, 3.0, threatened to lock us out from its great new features if somebody didn't step up and make our codebase compatible. GameDB specialist and dependency coordinator JordanTheToast – along with his emotional support TheTechnician27 – boldly took action. Days later, DEV9 expert and resident lifeguard AirGamer noticed both drowning in the deep end and decided to save them. Thanks to weeks of work by AirGamer, PCSX2 now uses SDL3. The SDL Wiki has <a href="https://wiki.libsdl.org/SDL3/NewFeatures">a comprehensive list</a> of new features, but for PCSX2, the immediate benefits are a cleaner API for easier development and the ability to utilize new features as they come. Special thanks goes to the unsung heroes at the SDL Wiki who wrote <a href="https://wiki.libsdl.org/SDL3/README-migration">this guide</a> and especially the authors of <a href="https://github.com/libsdl-org/SDL/blob/main/build-scripts/rename_symbols.py">this symbol rename script</a> who made this transition feasible.

<PCSX2PRLink authors="refractionpcsx2" prNums="11461">
  ### RT in RT Support (2)
</PCSX2PRLink>

For his capstone contribution to PCSX2 after over 20 years of unimaginable dedication, former lead developer refraction turned to his unfinished work on RT in RT support. Feeling duty-bound not to leave this to rot after he left, he agonized for months to get this working, and the end result is something to behold. First, though, let's address what this even <i>is</i>.

"RT in RT" stands for "<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_computer_graphics#render_target">render target</a> in render target". The PS2's <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphics_pipeline">graphics pipeline</a>, when turning its geometric shapes (called <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_primitive">primitives</a>) into a grid of pixels (<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rasterisation">rasterization</a>), sends data about these primitives to a "render target" such as a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Framebuffer">framebuffer</a> or a texture. Because the PS2's hardware incentivizes developers to do unspeakable things, we need to make an educated guess about the size and dimensions of what the game wants to draw and about what type of render target this data is being sent to.

This brings massive fixes to a huge variety of games – at minimum, multiple games with two-player splitscreen (such as <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Jak_X:_Combat_Racing">Jak X: Combat Racing</a></i>), multiple games that no longer need a hack to work (such as <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Ghost_in_the_Shell:_Stand_Alone_Complex">Ghost in the Shell: Stand Alone Complex</a></i>), multiple games whose effects now work (such as <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Drakengard">Drakengard</a></i>; comparison below), and multiple games that would only work before in software rendering (such as <i><a href="https://wiki.pcsx2.net/Hitman:_Contracts">Hitman: Contracts</a></i>; comparison below).
